## The Question I'm Exploring

Software engineering is being reinvented.

For decades, organisations have optimised how people build software. We created agile teams, product operating models, platform engineering practices, DevOps cultures, and cloud-native architectures.

Now AI is changing the fundamental economics of software delivery.

The question that drives my work is simple:

> **How should organisations redesign software engineering when AI fundamentally changes how software is built?**

I am CTO – Digital at The Lottery Corporation, where I lead the transformation of the digital organisation into a modern, product-led, AI-first engineering organisation.

Prior to this, I served as CTO for APAC at Thoughtworks, helping organisations navigate technology strategy, platform engineering, cloud transformation, and operating model change.

Today, my focus extends beyond technology itself. I'm interested in the systems that create technology: teams, incentives, culture, governance, leadership, and organisational design.

Because the biggest challenge of the AI era is not adopting new tools.

It's redesigning organisations to work differently.

Over the past two decades, I have worked across engineering, architecture, consulting, delivery, and executive technology leadership.

## The Principles That Guide My Thinking

### 1. Strategic Options Matter More Than Technology

I believe the ultimate measure of a CTO is not how many systems they build, but how many strategic options they create for the business.

We operate in an era of structural volatility where prediction is increasingly difficult and assumption half-lives continue to shrink. In that environment, the role of technology leadership is not to predict the future—it is to maximise the organisation's freedom to respond to it.

I think about this through a simple lens:

> **Strategic Options = Adaptive Capacity – Operational Gravity**

Adaptive capacity is created through architectural flexibility, engineering capability, organisational agility, and governance that enables rapid change.

Operational gravity emerges through technical debt, legacy coupling, organisational complexity, fragmented platforms, and maintenance burdens.

The role of technology leadership is to continuously expand adaptive capacity while relentlessly reducing operational gravity.

Every technology decision should ultimately increase the organisation's future freedom to manoeuvre.

### 2. Technology Excellence Creates Enduring Advantage

Technology excellence is not a benchmark to be achieved. It is a continuous pursuit.

I believe software engineering is a craft.

Excellent technology is not defined by the sophistication of the solution, but by its ability to solve meaningful problems while remaining resilient, scalable, maintainable, and adaptable to change.

Engineering excellence creates options. It allows organisations to move faster, respond more effectively to disruption, and deliver value with confidence.

### 3. AI Amplifies Human Capability

I believe Artificial Intelligence is a force multiplier for human ingenuity, not a replacement for it.

The greatest value of AI is not automation alone. It is the ability to reduce rework, improve quality, accelerate learning, and increase throughput across the software development lifecycle.

The most important AI questions are increasingly organisational rather than technical:

* How should teams be structured?
* How should engineering roles evolve?
* How should governance adapt?
* What becomes the role of leadership?

The future belongs to organisations that redesign how humans and AI collaborate.

### 4. Diversity Drives Better Outcomes

Diversity is not a metric to be achieved.

It is one of the most powerful sources of innovation, creativity, resilience, and empathy available to any organisation.

The best ideas emerge when people with different experiences, perspectives, backgrounds, and ways of thinking collaborate to solve complex problems.

Building inclusive environments is not simply the right thing to do. It is a strategic advantage.

### 5. Agility Is Organisational Adaptability

I do not view agility as a framework, methodology, or collection of ceremonies.

I view it as an organisational capability.

Software development is fundamentally a process of discovery. Teams rarely begin with complete information, and customer needs continually evolve.

The most successful organisations are those that can rapidly learn, adapt, and improve—continuously refining both the products they build and the systems used to build them.

### 6. Curiosity Expands What Is Possible

Curiosity is the foundation of innovation.

Progress begins with a question, not an answer.

The most effective leaders, technologists, and organisations remain students of the world around them. They challenge assumptions, seek diverse perspectives, explore uncertainty, and continuously learn.

Curiosity expands what is possible by helping us see opportunities that certainty often obscures.
